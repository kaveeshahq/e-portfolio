Target community

The targeted community for this field assignment was the Mediriwila Grama Niladhari Division adult community. This rural community was selected based on preliminary information gathered from key informants, suggesting potential nutritional deficiencies and limited awareness of healthy dietary practices among adults in the area.

Problem 1 — Low usage of protein sources

The dietary habit assessment revealed that 66.7% of community members rarely or never consumed sufficient protein-rich foods. Intake of plant-based protein sources such as legumes, dhal, and soy was particularly low. Key reasons identified include:

  • Limited knowledge about plant-based protein sources and their health benefits
  • Cultural preference for rice-centred meals with minimal protein accompaniments
  • Low awareness of daily protein requirements for adults
  • Misconception that only animal protein (meat/fish) is a valid protein source
  • Economic constraints limiting access to animal-sourced protein foods

Problem 2 — Low physical activity levels

The IPAQ (International Physical Activity Questionnaire) short form assessment showed that 70% of adults were sedentary and only 10% were sufficiently active. Contributing factors include:

  • Predominantly home-based daily routines with limited movement
  • Lack of knowledge about recommended physical activity levels for adults
  • Absence of structured physical activity facilities or programmes in the community
  • Cultural norms that do not prioritise exercise as part of daily life

Prioritised problems

Both low protein intake and low physical activity were prioritised as inter-related issues contributing to poor nutritional status and increased NCD risk among Mediriwila adults. The intervention was designed to address both problems together.

Activities and observations during 4 weeks

Week 1
Initial discussion with key informants
Selected the Mediriwila Grama Niladhari Division as the target community. Met with the Sanwardrana Niladhari (Community Development Officer) to gather background information on the community's health and nutrition situation. Visited the local Public Health Midwife (PHM) clinic to discuss common health concerns observed in the community. These meetings helped narrow down potential nutrition problems to investigate further and established good rapport with community gatekeepers.
Week 2
Problem identification — data collection
Conducted a comprehensive community assessment using three validated tools administered to adult community members: (1) the IPAQ Short Form to assess physical activity levels, (2) a socio-demographic questionnaire to collect background information on age, sex, occupation and household, and (3) a dietary habit assessment questionnaire developed in Sinhala via Google Forms. Anthropometric measurements were also taken including height, weight, waist and hip circumferences, and handgrip strength using a dynamometer. Data was compiled and analysed to confirm priority nutrition problems.
Week 3
Data analysis and intervention planning
Analysed all collected data confirming two priority problems: low protein source usage (66.7%) and low physical activity (70% sedentary). Began designing the multi-component intervention programme titled "නිරෝගී හෙතකට — සවිය දෙන පෝෂණය". Planned the programme structure covering drama, recipe guessing, physical activity, clay game, seed distribution, and a feedback session. Coordinated with the course coordinator Dr. Thilanka Ranathunga to confirm the programme date, time, and venue.
Week 4
Material preparation and programme implementation
Prepared all materials for the programme: crafted clay food models, prepared actual foods for the recipe guessing session, finalised recipe sheets, prepared seeds for distribution, wrote and rehearsed the drama script, and designed the Sinhala community notice. The full intervention programme was implemented at the Mediriwila Grama Sabha on 7 April at 10:00 AM. All six group members participated actively throughout.

The planned intervention

Programme concept

Based on the identified problems, a multi-component community nutrition intervention programme titled "නිරෝගී හෙතකට — සවිය දෙන පෝෂණය" (Nutrition for a Healthy Life) was designed for the Mediriwila adult community. The programme was planned to address both low protein intake and low physical activity simultaneously through participatory, culturally appropriate methods.

Rationale for this approach

A participatory, multi-method approach was chosen because the root cause of low protein intake in this community was a knowledge and awareness gap — not a food access issue. Adults in Mediriwila were simply unaware that locally available, affordable plant foods are rich protein sources. Interactive methods (drama, games, tasting) were preferred over lecture-based delivery to maximise engagement with an adult audience and improve retention of health messages.

Distributing seeds and protein-rich recipes was intentionally included as a practical, sustainable take-home action — moving the intervention beyond awareness toward real dietary behaviour change. The physical activity session addressed the second identified problem by demonstrating that exercise can be simple, enjoyable, and accessible for all adults.

Planned programme components

  • Drama performance — to open the session in a culturally engaging way, portraying everyday consequences of poor protein intake and inactivity
  • Recipe guessing session — using real foods prepared by the group to introduce high-protein plant-based recipes interactively
  • Physical activity session — a structured, adult-friendly exercise session designed to address low activity levels and demonstrate accessible movement
  • Clay food model identification game — an interactive game using handmade clay models to assess and reinforce knowledge of protein sources
  • Seed distribution — distributing seeds of protein-rich plants to encourage home growing and long-term dietary change
  • Recipe distribution — take-home recipe cards featuring affordable high-protein dishes using locally available ingredients
  • Feedback session — a structured evaluation at the close of the programme to assess participant understanding and gather feedback

Materials prepared for the intervention

Clay food models

Handmade clay models of multiple protein sources — fish, chicken, eggs, legumes, soy — painted and labelled in Sinhala. Used in the identification game to help participants recognise protein-rich foods in a tactile, visual way.

Recipe sheets

Take-home recipe cards featuring high-protein dishes prepared from affordable, locally available plant protein sources. Recipes were practically demonstrated during the session's recipe guessing activity.

Community notice (Sinhala)

A bilingual invitation poster designed in Sinhala and distributed in the community ahead of the programme. Stated the date (7 April, Wednesday), time (10:00 AM), and venue (Mediriwila Grama Sabha).

Drama script

A drama script written by group members portraying relatable village-life scenarios showing the consequences of insufficient protein intake and a sedentary lifestyle. Rehearsed multiple times before the programme day.

Seeds for distribution

Seeds of protein-rich plants selected for home growing in a typical rural household garden. Distributed to every participant as a practical, sustainable follow-up action to reinforce the session's key messages.

Feedback evaluation form

A dot-sticker feedback board designed to visually collect participant responses on whether the programme's messages were understood and to assess overall satisfaction with the intervention.

Programme setting

The intervention programme was held at the Mediriwila Grama Sabha (community hall) on 7 April (Wednesday) at 10:00 AM. A sufficient number of adult community members attended. All six group members participated actively throughout the entire programme.

What happened on the day — step by step

1
Drama performance — opening the session
The session was opened with the drama that group members had prepared and rehearsed. The drama depicted a relatable scenario from everyday Mediriwila village life — showing how a family's low protein intake and sedentary habits led to health problems, and how simple changes could make a difference. The drama immediately captured participants' attention, created laughter and engagement, and set a warm, participatory tone for the rest of the programme.
2
Recipe guessing session
Group members presented actual foods that had been prepared in advance and invited community participants to smell, observe, and guess the dishes and their key ingredients. This interactive tasting activity introduced high-protein plant-based recipes in an enjoyable, hands-on format, directly bridging the gap between unfamiliar protein-rich foods and familiar everyday cooking in the community.
3
Physical activity session
A structured physical activity session was conducted, specially designed and adapted for adults of various fitness levels. Simple, low-impact exercises were demonstrated by group members and participants were actively invited to join in. The session showed that physical activity does not require a gym or equipment — it can be done comfortably in everyday village settings — directly addressing the 70% sedentary rate found in the IPAQ assessment.
4
Clay food model identification game
The handmade clay food models were used in an interactive identification game. Community members were asked to identify which clay models represented protein-rich foods and to categorise them. The game evaluated whether participants had absorbed the key educational messages from the earlier parts of the session. Community members engaged enthusiastically, with many correctly identifying plant protein sources they previously had not recognised as "protein food."
5
Distribution of seeds and recipe sheets
Seeds of protein-rich plants were distributed to each participant to encourage home growing as a practical, sustainable long-term strategy for improving protein intake. Recipe sheets featuring affordable, high-protein dishes using locally available ingredients were also distributed as take-home resources, ensuring the session's messages could be applied in participants' daily home cooking.
6
Feedback session
A structured feedback session was conducted at the close of the programme using a dot-sticker board, where participants placed stickers to indicate their responses and level of understanding. Verbal feedback was also invited and documented. Participants gave positive feedback about the programme's content, the drama, and the interactive activities. The feedback confirmed that the key messages about plant protein sources and physical activity had been effectively communicated.

Evaluation: strengths, limitations and suggestions

Strengths of the intervention
  • Multi-method approach (drama, game, physical activity, tasting, seeds) sustained participant engagement from start to finish
  • Drama was highly culturally appropriate and instantly created rapport with the adult village community
  • Clay food models made learning visual and tactile — well suited for adults with varied literacy levels
  • Seed distribution moved the intervention beyond awareness to a practical, sustainable take-home action
  • Dot-sticker feedback board was a simple, inclusive evaluation method appropriate for a community setting
  • All six group members participated actively, ensuring professional and smooth delivery throughout
Weaknesses and limitations
  • A single one-time session is insufficient to produce sustained long-term behavioural change in protein dietary habits
  • No formal follow-up assessment was conducted to measure whether participants actually changed their protein consumption after the programme
  • Community sample size was limited, reducing generalisability of the identified problems to the broader population
  • IPAQ self-reported data is subject to recall bias and social desirability bias
  • Budget constraints (Rs. 4,000) limited the scale of seed distribution and quality of printed recipe materials
Suggestions to improve the intervention and problem identification
  • Conduct a follow-up session one month after the programme using the same dietary habit questionnaire to measure actual change in protein food consumption
  • Partner with the local PHM and Sanwardrana Niladhari to integrate the protein education messages into existing community health outreach for longer-term impact
  • Use pre- and post-knowledge tests on the day of the programme to objectively measure the immediate impact on participants' awareness
  • Expand the physical activity session into a regular community exercise group with volunteer leaders from within the community itself
  • Develop an illustrated bilingual (Sinhala/English) booklet covering protein sources, daily requirements, and simple recipes — a more durable resource than a single recipe sheet
  • Increase the sample size in the problem identification stage to include more households across the full Grama Niladhari division for more robust and representative data

Conclusion

This field assignment provided an invaluable opportunity to apply academic nutrition knowledge within a real community setting. Through systematic problem identification using validated tools — the IPAQ short form, dietary habit assessments, socio-demographic questionnaires, and anthropometric measurements including handgrip dynamometry — two inter-related priority problems were confirmed in the Mediriwila adult community: low usage of protein sources (66.7%) and low physical activity levels (70% sedentary).

A creative, multi-component intervention programme titled "නිරෝගී හෙතකට — සවිය දෙන පෝෂණය" was successfully designed and implemented at the Mediriwila Grama Sabha on 7 April. The programme combined a community drama performance, a recipe guessing session, a physical activity session, a clay food model identification game, seed and recipe distribution, and a structured feedback session — all within 45–60 minutes and within the allocated budget of Rs. 4,000.

The programme demonstrated that community nutrition education can be highly engaging, culturally sensitive, and practically actionable even within significant resource and time constraints. Participant feedback confirmed positive reception and increased awareness of plant protein sources and physical activity. While a single session has inherent limitations for producing lasting behavioural change, it laid an important foundation. Future efforts should incorporate follow-up sessions, community partnerships, and formal pre/post evaluation to sustain and measure impact.
